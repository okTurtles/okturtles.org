Never pay for an SSL certificate again. Paying a company to generate an SSL certificate for you is madness, especially when you're asked to pay every year and are given a certificate that doesn't do what it's supposed to do: provide your vistors with security. Companies that sell SSL certificates mislead their customers into believing that these certificates protect browser-server communication from all eavesdropping and tampering. As elaborated in our paper, this simply isn’t true today.

Say good-bye to identity theft and "domain seizures". Thanks to DNSNMC (Namecoin-powered DNS), your online identity actually belong to you. That is, when someone visits your website, they can be assured that it actually belongs to you, and won't be stolen from you1. When someone wants to send you a message, they don't have to worry that they're speaking with the wrong person, or that there's an invisible middle-man listening in to everything they're saying. Who's behind these projects?

okTurtles and DNSNMC are the brainchildren of Greg Slepak, but he did not invent every aspect of these concepts. He's grateful to Aaron Swartz for turning him on to the idea, and for answering some of his questions about it. Of course, none of this would be possible if it wasn't for Satoshi Nakamoto's historic paper on Bitcoin, and the entire Namecoin community.

Will Stanley is chiefly responsible for this website's design. He has a passion for clean, simple, beautiful design.

I want to help! How can I participate?

Certainly. You can participate directly (see the Get Involved link at the bottom of the page), or help fund the Kickstarter when it's ready (subscribe to know when that'll be).

Is this "vaporware"?

Absolutely not! We have a functioning (though incomplete) version of DNSNMC already written (it's less than a hundred lines of JavaScript to get a basic version running in Node.js!). Even if we were not able to complete this project ourselves (for whatever reason), the idea is so simple to implement that it is virtually guaranteed to happen. okTurtles is a tad more complicated and requires a community effort to keep it running smoothly on third-party sites, but it's readily doable. Most of the design has been explained in our paper, all that remains is to implement it. Github project pages will be brought online shortly!
